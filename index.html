Level 3 Activity Banks

For CEFR B1 Level | 15–25 minutes | Click a card to read

Vocabulary Games

Expand lexicon through synonyms, definitions, and collocations.

1

Word Splat

Goal: Identify words from definitions.

Students race to swat the correct vocabulary word after hearing its definition or synonym.

Read more
2 clean flyswatters, whiteboard markers.
  1. Write 15-20 target vocabulary words randomly on the whiteboard.
  2. Divide the students into two teams and invite one representative from each team to the front.
  3. Provide a clear definition, a synonym, or an example sentence with a blank (e.g., "A word that means extremely tired").
  4. The first student to swat the correct word (e.g., "exhausted") earns a point for their team.
Do not translate the words. Force students to process English definitions to improve their contextual understanding.
2

Taboo

Goal: Describe words using circumlocution.

Students describe a target word without using three specific "forbidden" related words.

Read more
Custom Taboo cards (a target word + 3 forbidden words).
  1. Divide the class into teams. One student comes to the front and picks a card.
  2. The card has a target word (e.g., "Airport") and three forbidden words (e.g., "Plane", "Fly", "Travel").
  3. The student must describe the target word to their team without using any of the forbidden words.
  4. The team has one minute to guess as many words as possible.
This activity perfectly trains B1 learners to use relative clauses (e.g., "It's a place where you go when you want to visit another country").
3

Synonym Bingo

Goal: Expand synonymous vocabulary.

Students mark words on their bingo grids based on the synonyms called out by the teacher.

Read more
Blank paper, pens, a list of target words and their synonyms.
  1. Write 16 target words on the whiteboard.
  2. Students draw a 3x3 grid and fill it with 9 words of their choice from the board.
  3. Instead of calling out the exact words on the board, call out their synonyms or antonyms (e.g., say "Wealthy" for "Rich").
  4. Students cross out the corresponding word. The first to complete a line shouts "Bingo!".
Have the winning student read back the words they crossed out, and ask them to remind the class which synonym you used for each.
4

Word Association

Goal: Think critically about lexical sets.

Students form a continuous chain of words where each word must logically relate to the previous one.

Read more
A soft ball or timer.
  1. Have students stand in a circle.
  2. The teacher says a starting word (e.g., "Ocean") and tosses the ball to a student.
  3. The student must say a related word (e.g., "Waves") within 3 seconds and toss the ball to someone else.
  4. If a student hesitates too long or says an unrelated word, they must quickly justify the connection or they sit down.
Encourage abstract thinking. If someone links "Ocean" to "Plastic", it's a great opportunity to briefly discuss environmental vocabulary.
5

Story Snowball

Goal: Use vocabulary in context.

Students collaboratively write a story by throwing "snowballs" and adding sentences using target words.

Read more
A4 paper, pens, a list of target words on the board.
  1. Each student writes a starting sentence incorporating one vocabulary word from the board.
  2. They crumple the paper into a snowball and throw it across the room.
  3. Everyone picks up a new snowball, reads the sentence, and adds a second sentence using a *different* vocabulary word.
  4. Repeat this process 4-5 times, then have students read the completed, often humorous, stories aloud.
To maintain a cohesive narrative, remind students to use appropriate linking words (however, suddenly, eventually) when adding their sentences.
6

Twenty Questions

Goal: Formulate indirect questions.

The class asks up to 20 Yes/No questions to deduce the secret vocabulary word held by a student.

Read more
Vocabulary flashcards.
  1. One student selects a vocabulary card and keeps it hidden from the class.
  2. The rest of the class must ask Yes/No questions to gather clues (e.g., "Is it an adjective?", "Can you find it in an office?").
  3. The student at the front can only answer "Yes", "No", or "Sometimes".
  4. The class wins if they guess the exact word before asking 20 questions.
Provide a scaffold on the board for question types (Categories, Locations, Adjectives, Actions) to help them formulate strategic questions.
7

Collocation Match

Goal: Learn natural word combinations.

Students match verbs to nouns, or adjectives to prepositions to form natural English collocations.

Read more
Cards split into collocation halves (e.g., "Make" / "a mistake").
  1. Scatter half-cards face down on a large table or stick them to the whiteboard.
  2. Divide the class into pairs.
  3. Pairs take turns flipping two cards. They must determine if the two words naturally collocate (e.g., "Interested" + "in").
  4. If they form a correct collocation, the pair must use it in a sentence to keep the cards.
Focus heavily on tricky verb collocations (Make vs. Do, Have vs. Take) which are a core part of the B1 curriculum.
8

Category Race

Goal: Brainstorm vocabulary rapidly.

Teams race in a relay format to write as many words as possible belonging to a specific topic.

Read more
Whiteboard divided into sections, markers.
  1. Divide the whiteboard into columns for each team and form relay lines.
  2. Announce a B1-level category (e.g., "Occupations", "Emotions", or "Things in a kitchen").
  3. On "Go!", the first student runs up, writes one relevant word, and passes the marker to the next teammate.
  4. After 2 minutes, the team with the most correctly spelled and relevant words wins.
To challenge B1 learners, use grammatical categories instead of just nouns (e.g., "Irregular Past Participles" or "Adjectives ending in -ed").

Grammar Games

Master complex structures, conditionals, and tenses.

1

Clause Connection

Goal: Link ideas logically.

Groups combine independent and dependent clauses using appropriate conjunctions.

Read more
Cards with clauses, cards with conjunctions (although, despite, unless).
  1. Give each group a set of clause cards and a set of conjunction cards.
  2. Students must logically match two clauses and choose the correct conjunction to link them.
  3. For example, they connect "It was raining heavily" and "We went for a walk" using "Even though".
  4. The first group to connect all clauses correctly wins.
Review the punctuation rules before starting. Remind them when to use a comma if the dependent clause comes first.
2

Experience Mingle

Goal: Practice the Present Perfect.

Students mingle to ask "Have you ever...?" and follow up with past simple questions.

Read more
"Find Someone Who" worksheets, pens.
  1. Provide a worksheet with prompts like "Find someone who has eaten something unusual."
  2. Students mingle and form Present Perfect questions: "Have you ever eaten something unusual?"
  3. If a classmate answers "Yes, I have," the student MUST ask a follow-up question using Past Simple: "What did you eat? When did you eat it?"
  4. Students record the answers and report back to the class.
This is the ideal activity to emphasize the shift from general experience (Present Perfect) to specific details (Past Simple).
3

Conditional Tic-Tac-Toe

Goal: Master 1st and 2nd conditionals.

Teams form accurate conditional sentences to claim squares on the grid.

Read more
Whiteboard, markers.
  1. Draw a 3x3 grid. Write prompts inside (e.g., "win the lottery", "rain tomorrow").
  2. Divide the class into Team X and Team O.
  3. A team selects a square and must form a complete 1st or 2nd conditional sentence based on the prompt.
  4. For example: "If I won the lottery, I would travel the world."
  5. If grammatically flawless, they claim the square.
Pay close attention to their verb forms. Reject the sentence immediately if they say "If I will rain" instead of "If it rains".
4

Transformation Race

Goal: Manipulate sentence structures.

Relay race where students convert sentences from active to passive voice, or direct to reported speech.

Read more
Whiteboard, 2 markers, list of sentences.
  1. Divide the whiteboard into two sections for two competing teams.
  2. Write an active sentence on the board (e.g., "Shakespeare wrote Hamlet").
  3. One student from each team races to rewrite the sentence in the passive voice ("Hamlet was written by Shakespeare").
  4. The first team to write the transformed sentence perfectly wins a point.
Make sure to include sentences in different tenses (past, present, future) to fully test their grasp of the passive voice rules.
5

Elaboration Ball

Goal: Expand responses naturally.

Students catch the ball, answer a question, and must add at least two supporting details.

Read more
A soft ball.
  1. Students stand in a circle. Throw the ball to a student and ask a broad question (e.g., "What is your favorite movie?").
  2. The student must state their answer AND provide at least two reasons or details using complex sentences.
  3. For example: "My favorite movie is Inception because the plot is very complex, and the visual effects are stunning."
  4. They then throw the ball to a peer and ask a new question.
B1 learners often give short answers. This game forces them to break that habit and practice conversational fluency and elaboration.
6

Grammar Auction

Goal: Analyze and evaluate grammar.

Teams analyze sentences with subtle B1-level errors and bid on the ones they believe are correct.

Read more
Fake money, a list of 10-12 sentences projected on the board.
  1. Project 10 sentences. Embed subtle errors in some of them (e.g., using "since" instead of "for", or dropping an article).
  2. Give teams $1000 in fake money and 5 minutes to privately discuss the sentences.
  3. Act as an auctioneer. Read each sentence and let teams bid to "buy" it.
  4. Reveal the corrections at the end. The team that purchased the most grammatically correct sentences wins.
Include common mistakes your specific students make frequently. It acts as an excellent, engaging review session.
7

Relative Clause Chain

Goal: Use relative pronouns effectively.

Students build a continuous story by extending the previous sentence with a relative clause.

Read more
None.
  1. Provide a base sentence: "I met a man."
  2. The next student adds a defining relative clause: "I met a man who was wearing a red hat."
  3. The next student extends it further: "I met a man who was wearing a red hat, which he bought in Paris."
  4. Continue until the sentence becomes too difficult to remember, then start a new one.
This requires intense concentration. Write the growing sentence on the board so students can visually analyze how clauses connect to nouns.
8

Paragraph Ordering

Goal: Understand cohesion and transition.

Groups arrange mixed sentences to form a coherent paragraph based on transition words.

Read more
A short text cut into individual sentences.
  1. Take a cohesive paragraph (e.g., an opinion essay or a process description) and cut it into separate sentences.
  2. Give the mixed pile to small groups.
  3. Students must identify linking words (however, furthermore, as a result) and pronoun references to reconstruct the paragraph perfectly.
After they finish, ask them to highlight the specific transition words that helped them deduce the correct order.

Conversation Games

Negotiate, debate, and express opinions fluidly.

1

Debate Carousel

Goal: Discuss and justify opinions.

Students discuss engaging B1 topics with a partner, then rotate to share their views with someone else.

Read more
A list of engaging discussion prompts (e.g., technology, environment).
  1. Form an inner circle and an outer circle facing each other.
  2. Provide a prompt (e.g., "Is social media more harmful than helpful?").
  3. Pairs discuss and justify their opinions for 2 minutes.
  4. Call "Rotate!". The outer circle moves to the right, and they discuss a new prompt with a new partner.
Teach students phrases for agreeing and disagreeing respectfully (e.g., "I see your point, but..." or "I completely agree because...") before starting.
2

Holiday Planning

Goal: Negotiate and compromise.

Partners must negotiate and agree on details for a theoretical trip using specific constraints.

Read more
Role cards with conflicting preferences (e.g., one wants the beach, one wants a city tour).
  1. Put students in pairs. Tell them they have $2000 to plan a one-week holiday.
  2. Give them secret profile cards. Partner A prefers nature and relaxing. Partner B prefers museums and nightlife.
  3. They must discuss, negotiate, and compromise to create an itinerary that satisfies both.
  4. Pairs present their final holiday plan to the class.
This activity excellently practices modals of suggestion and advice ("We could go to...", "What if we...", "I would rather...").
3

Problem Roleplay

Goal: Handle complex situations.

Students act out difficult real-life scenarios, like making a complaint at a hotel or returning a broken item.

Read more
Scenario cards detailing specific problems.
  1. Distribute scenario cards to pairs. (Example: "You are at a restaurant. There is a bug in your soup, but the waiter doesn't believe you.")
  2. Give them 3 minutes to prepare their arguments and responses.
  3. Pairs perform their unscripted dialogue in front of the class.
  4. The class evaluates who had the more persuasive argument.
Encourage students not to script every single word. They should focus on the main points and react naturally to what their partner says.
4

Two Truths & A Lie

Goal: Use narrative tenses.

Students share experiences using past continuous and past simple, while the class tries to spot the lie.

Read more
Paper, pens.
  1. Students write three detailed sentences about past experiences. (e.g., "While I was traveling in Japan, I lost my passport.")
  2. Two experiences must be real, and one must be entirely fictional.
  3. A student reads their statements. The class aggressively asks detailed follow-up questions to catch them lying.
  4. The class votes on which statement is the lie.
Require students to use at least one complex sentence (using while/when) to ensure they are practicing B1 level grammar, not just basic past tense.
5

Proverb Match

Goal: Understand idiomatic language.

Mingle to match halves of common English expressions, then discuss their meanings.

Read more
Common idioms/proverbs cut in half.
  1. Cut common B1-level expressions in half (e.g., "A piece of" / "cake", or "Cost an arm" / "and a leg").
  2. Distribute one half to each student.
  3. Students mingle, reading their halves to find their logical match.
  4. Once paired, they sit together and must think of a real-life situation where they would use this phrase to present to the class.
Do not let them show the text to each other. Emphasize that idioms often don't make literal sense, which tests their cultural and contextual understanding.
6

Opinion Jenga

Goal: Articulate thoughts spontaneously.

Pull a block and speak for a full minute on a philosophical or opinion-based question.

Read more
Jenga game, numbered list of opinion prompts.
  1. Number the blocks of a Jenga game. Prepare a list of prompts like "What is your opinion on artificial intelligence?" or "Is a university degree necessary?".
  2. A student pulls a block and checks the corresponding question.
  3. They must provide a structured response (Introduction, Reason 1, Reason 2, Conclusion) without letting the tower fall.
Teach students "filler phrases" (e.g., "That's an interesting question," or "Let me think about that for a second") to help them buy time while formulating their thoughts.
7

Press Conference

Goal: Practice formal inquiry and response.

One student plays an "expert" while journalists ask probing, formal questions.

Read more
Role cards with complex identities (e.g., The Mayor, A controversial scientist).
  1. Assign one student a high-profile role. They sit at the front of the room.
  2. The class acts as investigative journalists. They must formulate polite but challenging questions (e.g., "Could you explain why you decided to...?").
  3. The "expert" must defend their actions and policies.
  4. Rotate the role after a dynamic 3-minute interrogation.
Require the journalists to use indirect questions (e.g., "I was wondering if you could tell us...") to practice formal register.
8

Devil's Advocate

Goal: Develop counter-arguments.

Students must listen to their partner's opinion and construct a logical argument for the exact opposite side.

Read more
None.
  1. Put students in pairs. Student A states a strong personal opinion (e.g., "I believe public transport should be free").
  2. Regardless of their actual personal belief, Student B must act as the "Devil's Advocate" and argue against it.
  3. Student B provides two structured reasons why Student A is wrong.
  4. They debate for 2 minutes, then switch roles with a new topic.
This builds immense cognitive flexibility in English. Remind them to focus on the structure of the argument, not just arguing for the sake of it.

Reading Games

Enhance skimming, scanning, and analytical comprehension.

1

Article Jigsaw

Goal: Summarize detailed texts.

Each student reads a segment of a news article and summarizes the main points for their group.

Read more
A B1-level article divided into 3-4 sections.
  1. Take an interesting news article and cut it into distinct sections.
  2. Form groups. Give each member a different section to read silently.
  3. They must extract the main idea and key details without translating word-for-word.
  4. Students take turns orally summarizing their sections so the group can comprehend the entire article.
Give the groups a set of overarching comprehension questions that can only be answered if all members communicate their summaries accurately.
2

Dictogloss

Goal: Reconstruct textual meaning.

Students listen to a text, take notes, and work together to rewrite it as close to the original as possible.

Read more
A short, grammar-rich text, paper, pens.
  1. Read a short paragraph aloud at a normal speaking pace twice.
  2. Students listen and quickly jot down keywords (nouns, verbs). They should not try to write every word.
  3. In pairs or small groups, they pool their notes and reconstruct the paragraph grammatically.
  4. Compare the students' texts with the original projected on the board.
Focus on the *meaning* rather than exact replication. If they used a valid synonym or alternative grammar structure, praise it!
3

Read and Map

Goal: Visualize text structures.

Students read a factual text and translate the information into a detailed mind map or flowchart.

Read more
Informational text (e.g., an article on how coffee is made), large poster paper, markers.
  1. Provide a text that explains a process, a historical event, or categorization.
  2. Students read the text independently to grasp the core concepts.
  3. In groups, they must visually represent the text's relationships by drawing a mind map or flowchart.
  4. Groups present their visual maps to the class, explaining the connections.
This trains students to identify main ideas versus supporting details, a crucial skill for B1 reading comprehension tests.
4

Heading Match

Goal: Identify paragraph main ideas.

Students skim several paragraphs and match them to the most appropriate summary heading.

Read more
An article with paragraphs labeled A, B, C; a separate list of headings.
  1. Provide an article where the headings have been removed and mixed up in a separate list. Include one extra "distractor" heading.
  2. Students skim the paragraphs, focusing on topic sentences.
  3. They assign the correct heading to each paragraph based on the overall theme.
  4. Discuss the clues that led them to their answers.
This mimics typical B1 exam tasks (like IELTS or Cambridge PET). Teach them to read the first and last sentence of a paragraph carefully.
5

Fact or Opinion

Goal: Analyze author intent.

Students analyze a text to distinguish between objective facts and the author's subjective opinions.

Read more
A review or editorial article, highlighters.
  1. Give students an opinionated text, like a restaurant review or an editorial on climate change.
  2. Ask them to highlight undeniable facts in yellow, and subjective opinions in pink.
  3. Discuss specific sentences as a class. For example, "The movie lasted 2 hours" (Fact) vs. "The movie felt too long" (Opinion).
Point out signal words that often introduce opinions, such as "believe," "suggests," "unfortunately," or subjective adjectives like "terrible" or "beautiful".
6

Mystery Sequencing

Goal: Deduce narrative chronologies.

Groups reconstruct a complex narrative or news report from scrambled paragraphs.

Read more
A narrative story cut into paragraphs.
  1. Take a mystery story or a timeline-based news report and cut it into paragraphs.
  2. Mix them up and hand them to small groups.
  3. Students must read carefully to deduce the logical flow based on pronouns (he, it), time markers (previously, subsequently), and cause-and-effect.
  4. The first group to order the text correctly wins.
If they get stuck, ask them to find the "hook" (the introduction) and the "conclusion" first to establish the boundaries of the text.
7

Info Scavenger Hunt

Goal: Scan for specific data rapidly.

Students scan authentic materials like brochures or websites to locate specific details.

Read more
Printed tourist brochures, train schedules, or website printouts. Worksheet with specific questions.
  1. Distribute authentic English reading materials (e.g., a museum brochure).
  2. Provide a worksheet with highly specific questions (e.g., "How much is a student ticket on Tuesdays?", "What time does the cafe close?").
  3. Students race to scan the documents, locate the data, and write the answers.
Teach scanning techniques: tell them to stop reading every word and let their eyes jump around looking for capital letters, symbols ($), or numbers.
8

Advanced Cloze

Goal: Apply structural grammar cues.

Students fill in blanks focusing on prepositions, articles, and conjunctions without a word bank.

Read more
A cloze text with functional words removed.
  1. Provide a text where structural words (in, on, at, the, however, because) have been blanked out. Do NOT provide a word bank.
  2. Students must read the sentence and rely entirely on their grammatical knowledge and context to determine what type of word fits.
  3. Review the answers as a class, discussing why certain words are grammatically required.
This is excellent preparation for standardized tests. Remind them to look at the word immediately preceding and following the blank to determine the part of speech needed.

All Skills Review

Integrate reading, writing, listening, and speaking into holistic tasks.

1

Jeopardy (B1 Edition)

Goal: Comprehensive review.

A quiz show format testing phrasal verbs, idioms, advanced grammar, and speaking fluency.

Read more
Whiteboard or Presentation template with categories and point values.
  1. Draw a grid with B1 categories (e.g., Phrasal Verbs, Conditionals, Error Correction, Give an Opinion).
  2. Assign point values from 100 to 500 based on difficulty.
  3. Teams select a category. For a 500-point Speaking task, they might have to talk about a topic continuously for 45 seconds without pausing.
  4. If they succeed, they win points; if they fail, another team can steal.
Incorporate questions from previous lessons to ensure it acts as a genuine spaced-repetition review.
2

Task Stations

Goal: Independent multi-skill practice.

Groups rotate through stations designed to independently test distinct language modalities.

Read more
4 distinct activity setups around the room.
  1. Set up 4 stations: Reading (analyze a short article), Writing (draft an email complaint), Speaking (record a 1-minute voice note answering a prompt), Listening (listen to a short podcast snippet and answer questions).
  2. Divide the class into groups and assign them a starting station.
  3. Give groups 8-10 minutes per station before blowing a whistle to rotate.
This requires heavy front-loading. Ensure instructions at each station are explicitly clear so you act purely as a facilitator, not an explainer.
3

Discussion Board Game

Goal: Foster extended speaking.

Students navigate a board game where every square requires extended, unscripted speech on complex topics.

Read more
Printed board games, dice, tokens.
  1. Provide a board game to small groups. Squares should contain prompts like "Describe a time you solved a difficult problem" or "Debate the pros/cons of fast food."
  2. Students roll the dice and move their token.
  3. They must speak fluently on the prompt for at least 45 seconds to remain on the square.
Encourage group members to ask follow-up questions to keep the speaker talking if they struggle to fill the time limit.
4

Design a Product

Goal: Project-based collaboration.

Groups invent a new product, write a marketing pitch, and present it to persuade the class.

Read more
Large paper, markers, brainstorming worksheets.
  1. Task groups with inventing a product that solves an everyday problem.
  2. They must sketch the product, label its features (Vocabulary), and write a persuasive 1-minute pitch (Writing/Grammar).
  3. Groups present their product to the class (Speaking).
  4. The class asks questions regarding the product's functionality (Listening/Speaking).
Provide useful persuasive structures like "Have you ever struggled with...?", "Our product guarantees...", and "Unlike competitors, it...".
5

Mystery Escape

Goal: Solve analytical language puzzles.

Teams decrypt B1-level riddles, advanced crosswords, and reading logic puzzles to 'escape'.

Read more
Sequential envelopes containing challenging language tasks.
  1. Prepare puzzles that require true language comprehension (e.g., deciphering a code based on verb conjugations, reading a schedule to find a hidden time).
  2. Teams solve the puzzle in Envelope 1 to discover the password for Envelope 2.
  3. The first team to analytically work through all envelopes "escapes" the room.
Make the puzzles sequential. For example, the answer to the reading puzzle is a word they need to use in the grammar puzzle.
6

The Job Interview

Goal: Roleplay professional interactions.

Students simulate job interviews, evaluating candidates based on resumes and professional inquiry.

Read more
Mock job descriptions, simplified CVs.
  1. Divide the class into "Employers" and "Candidates". Give employers a job description, and give candidates a mock CV detailing their experience.
  2. Employers ask formal questions: "Can you tell me about your experience?", "What are your strengths?".
  3. Candidates must respond professionally, expanding on their provided CV.
  4. Rotate roles after 10 minutes.
This is highly relevant for B1 learners entering the workforce. Review formal vocabulary ("qualifications", "assets", "responsible for") beforehand.
7

Consequences (Writing)

Goal: Practice narrative tenses seamlessly.

A classic collaborative writing game focusing on linking past simple and past continuous.

Read more
Paper, pens.
  1. Prompt students to write "Who" at the top of the paper, fold it over to hide the text, and pass it.
  2. Next, write "Met Whom", fold, and pass.
  3. Continue with: "Where", "What they were doing" (Past Continuous), "What happened next" (Past Simple), and "The outcome".
  4. Unfold the papers and read the generated stories aloud.
This is excellent for highlighting how different past tenses function together in storytelling (setting the scene vs. the interrupting action).
8

Error Correction Relay

Goal: Peer edit and refine texts.

Teams race to identify and correct grammatical and lexical errors embedded in a long text.

Read more
A printed text with 10-15 specific errors, red pens.
  1. Place a text full of typical B1 errors (wrong prepositions, tense mismatches, subject-verb agreement) at the front of the room.
  2. The first student from each team runs up, finds ONE error, crosses it out, writes the correction, and runs back.
  3. If a subsequent runner spots an invalid correction made by their teammate, they can fix it.
  4. The first team to submit a perfectly corrected text wins.
Ensure the errors are realistic mistakes your students frequently make in their essays, providing targeted, dynamic remediation.

Listening Games

Train cognitive focus, dictation, and detail extraction.

1

Listen and Sketch

Goal: Map spatial relationships.

Students listen to a detailed spatial description of a town layout or house plan and draw it.

Read more
Blank paper, pens.
  1. Prepare a detailed description (e.g., "The bank is opposite the station, and the bakery is nestled between the post office and the gym").
  2. Read the description aloud at a natural pace.
  3. Students must process the prepositions of place and sketch an accurate map.
  4. Compare their maps with the actual plan projected on the board.
Use advanced prepositions (opposite, adjacent to, parallel to, on the corner of) to elevate this beyond basic listening.
2

Listen for the Mistake

Goal: Maintain auditory focus.

Students read a text, then listen to the teacher tell the story and must shout to identify factual errors.

Read more
A short printed text for students, an altered script for the teacher.
  1. Provide students with a printed text. Give them a minute to scan it.
  2. Read the story aloud, but subtly change key facts, numbers, or adjectives (e.g., read "1995" instead of "1985").
  3. When students hear a discrepancy, they must shout "Mistake!" and provide the correct information from their text.
Change subtle grammatical details as well (like reading "he has gone" instead of "he had gone") to test intensive listening.
3

Slap the Synonym

Goal: Process auditory semantics.

Students listen to a word and must quickly slap the card displaying its synonym.

Read more
Word cards with target vocabulary spread on a table.
  1. Scatter vocabulary cards (e.g., "Huge", "Wealthy", "Furious") face up on the table.
  2. Call out a synonym (e.g., say "Big", "Rich", or "Angry").
  3. Students must mentally translate the concept and slap the corresponding synonym card.
  4. The student who collects the most cards wins.
This demands rapid cognitive processing, moving beyond simple word recognition to actual semantic understanding.
4

Message Relay

Goal: Extract and transmit key data.

Students simulate passing a complex phone message to an executive "secretary".

Read more
Scripts of phone messages.
  1. Divide the class into teams of three: the Caller, the Messenger, and the Secretary.
  2. The Caller reads a complex message (e.g., "Tell Mr. Smith his flight is delayed to 4 PM and he needs to call the London office.") to the Messenger.
  3. The Messenger runs to the Secretary and relays the information from memory.
  4. The Secretary writes it down. Check for accuracy against the original.
Introduce distractors in the phone message (e.g., "His flight was at 2 PM, but now it's delayed to 4 PM") to force the listener to filter out irrelevant information.
5

Listen and Complete

Goal: Complete data charts accurately.

Students listen to an audio track or teacher reading, filling in missing data on a schedule or form.

Read more
Blank schedules or forms (e.g., train timetables, patient intake forms).
  1. Distribute a form with missing information fields (e.g., Name, Departure Time, Destination).
  2. Play an audio track or read a dialogue aloud naturally (e.g., purchasing a train ticket at a kiosk).
  3. Students must isolate the specific requested information and fill in the form.
  4. Review the completed forms together.
This perfectly mimics standard listening exams (like TOEIC or IELTS). Instruct students to scan the form beforehand to anticipate what kind of data (numbers, names) they need to listen for.
6

Action Chain

Goal: Retain complex multi-step instructions.

Students must remember and perform a sequence of 3 to 4 specific instructions in chronological order.

Read more
None.
  1. Provide a chain of instructions quickly without repeating: "Pick up your pen, tap it twice on the desk, stand up, and say your name."
  2. Call on a specific student to execute the entire sequence flawlessly.
  3. If they miss a step or perform them out of order, they are eliminated.
Use complex vocabulary ("tap", "fold", "slide") and conditionals ("If you are wearing blue, clap your hands; otherwise, snap your fingers") to elevate the difficulty.
7

Song Dictation

Goal: Understand connected speech.

Students listen to a pop song and transcribe entire missing phrases or idioms, not just single words.

Read more
Song lyrics with missing chunks, music player.
  1. Print out song lyrics but leave entire phrases or clauses blank (e.g., "____________, but I still haven't found what I'm looking for").
  2. Play the song chunk by chunk.
  3. Students listen and transcribe the missing segments.
  4. Analyze the connected speech (how words blend together) during the review.
Select songs that feature idioms or B1-level phrasal verbs. Discuss how the singer links words naturally to improve students' own pronunciation.
8

Blindfold Navigator

Goal: Use precise directional vocabulary.

Students verbally guide a blindfolded partner through a classroom obstacle course.

Read more
Blindfolds, harmless obstacles (chairs, bags).
  1. Create a safe obstacle course in the classroom using chairs and desks.
  2. Pair students up; one is blindfolded, the other is the Navigator.
  3. The Navigator must give precise verbal commands (e.g., "Take two steps forward", "Turn 90 degrees to your left") to guide their partner safely across the room.
Restrict pointing or touching. Success must rely entirely on the Navigator's precise English and the listener's accurate comprehension.
